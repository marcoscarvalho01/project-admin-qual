PROJECT OVERVIEW

Welcome to project Midas!

The goal of this project is to create a Golden Dataset that will be used to evaluate how well models perform on answering document-based questions.

Your goal in this task is to write a factual, financial-related prompt based on multiple 10-K/10-Q SEC filings from pre-assigned companies, answer the prompt you've created, and write the supporting facts from the documents. A typical task workflow will be:

  1. Review the pre-assigned companies that belong to your assigned industry segment.

  3. Write a Multi-Document Complex Prompt whose answer MUST be present in and require the use of all the documents.
  4. Write a Perfect Answer that addresses your prompt.
  5. Write all the Supporting Facts that were used to write your Perfect Answer, tag exactly which document they belong to, and list their full Location in the document.
  6. Write a Rationale to provide all steps taken to get to your Perfect Answer, explicitly referencing your sources.

VERY IMPORTANT: ONLY USE SEC FILINGS PUBLISHED FROM OCTOBER 2023 OR MORE RECENT.

DO NOT INCLUDE ANY PERSONALLY IDENTIFIABLE INFORMATION (PII) in ANY portion of your Prompt or Answer. SEC filings are public, heavily regulated documents that intentionally disclose the names, salaries, and backgrounds of top executives. Therefore, not all names are considered prohibited PII. The goal of your PII restriction is to prevent the AI from memorizing and later generating sensitive personal data of ordinary individuals or private contact information.

❌ STRICTLY PROHIBITED (Treat as PII)

  • Direct email addresses (e.g., "j.smith@company.com")
  • Direct phone numbers or personal extensions
  • Home addresses or private physical locations (even if tangentially mentioned in legal proceedings)
  • Names of ordinary employees, middle managers, or rank-and-file workers.
  • Names of whistleblowers or plaintiffs in HR-related lawsuits against the company.
  • Names of individual retail investors mentioned in proxy contexts or minority shareholder lawsuits.
  • Account numbers, Social Security Numbers (SSN), or tax IDs (which should never be in a 10-K anyway, but sometimes leak in attached exhibits).
  • Specific medical conditions or health statuses of executives (e.g., if a CEO takes a leave of absence and the 10-K names the specific illness).
  • Details about an executive's family members (spouses, children) sometimes mentioned in related-party transaction disclosures.

✅ PERMITTED (Do NOT Treat as PII)

Because this is a financial analysis project, restricting all names would make the dataset useless. You are allowed to use the following, as they are public, material facts to the business:

  • Names of the CEO, CFO, COO, CTO, etc. (e.g., Tim Cook, Satya Nadella).
  • Names of the Board of Directors.
  • Salaries, stock options, and bonuses of the named executive officers (NEOs) explicitly detailed in the filing.
  • Names of external auditing firms (e.g., PwC, Deloitte).
  • Names of major institutional shareholders (e.g., Vanguard, BlackRock) or partner companies.
  • General corporate contact information (e.g., the company's main HQ address or the generic investor_relations@company.com inbox).

WRITING FACTUAL PROMPTS

A good prompt is the heart of this project, so let's make sure yours is built to last! The goal here is to write the kind of financial-related rigorous question that a real analyst would ask before or while reviewing an SEC filing. It must be atomic, specific, grounded in the document, and with one clear correct answer (the Ground Truth).

Your prompt MUST:

  • Be atomic. This means your prompt must contain a SINGLE question only.
  • Be strictly financial or accounting-based. Focus on revenues, margins, cash flows, debt structures, asset turnover, tax reconciliations, company/segment performance, not on opinions.
  • Be natural and realistic, as if you were asking a question to a fellow finance analyst.
  • Be grounded in the documents, answerable using ONLY the SEC documents you uploaded.
  • Be factual, that is, ask for factual financial data, such as numbers, percentages, or accounting policies.
  • Be specific enough that there is only one correct mathematical or accounting answer. Do not ask vague questions, such as "How did the companies' finances perform?"

Your prompt MUST NOT:

  • Focus on non-financial trivia (e.g., "How many employees do the companies have?", "Where are the headquarters located?" - these do not test the AI's financial reasoning).
  • Ask for investment advice or stock predictions or opinion (e.g., "Which company is a better buy based on its P/E ratio?").
  • Be answerable from general financial knowledge alone without reading the filing. (e.g., "What is the formula for Free Cash Flow?").
  • Target any images. They must only target selectable texts, tables, paragraphs, etc.
  • Include any personally identifiable information (PII).

This is especially important: UNDER ANY CIRCUMSTANCES, DO NOT RE-USE PROMPTS. The purpose of this project is to create a high-quality DIVERSE data-set, so submissions from the same worker where the prompts are equal or very similar will be flagged as BAD and you will be removed from the project.

MULTI-DOCUMENT COMPLEX PROMPTS

In this prompt category, you are exclusively writing Complex Prompts that span across multiple documents. A Multi-Document Complex Prompt is a question that requires extracting, inferring, and synthesizing information from at least two different sources to form a single calculation or analytical conclusion.

What makes a great Multi-Document Complex Prompt?

A great Multi-Document prompt must fundamentally require the reader to open, read, and pull data from ALL uploaded documents to calculate the final answer. If the question can be answered using a smaller amount of documents than the ones assigned, the task is an automatic failure!

The Multi-Doc Test: Before submitting a Complex Prompt, ask yourself:

  • Does answering this require finding at least one distinct variable in EACH assigned document?
  • Does it ask for a delta, a ratio, an average, or a combined total across the filings?
  • Would a financial professional get this right while a non-specialist might make a mistake?

If you answered yes to these, that's a top-tier Complex Prompt!

WRITING PERFECT ANSWERS

Alright! Now that you have a Perfect Prompt, it's time to write the Perfect Answer. Think of this as the ultimate "answer key". In the future, this exact text will be used as the Ground Truth for other raters to evaluate whether the model's answer is correct or not.

Your answer should be exactly how you would want a highly competent fellow financial analyst to reply to your question.

What makes a Perfect Answer?

  1. Be direct and concise
    Answer the question immediately. Do not add conversational fluff, filler words, or extra information that the prompt didn't ask for. If the prompt asks for a difference in revenue, do not include their respective net incomes!
    ❌ Bad: "Well, Apple made $100M and Microsoft made $80M, so the difference is $20M which is interesting." (Too conversational, includes unnecessary extra facts).
    ✅ Good: "The absolute difference in operating income was $20 million."
  2. Use full sentences
    While we want you to be concise, the answer must still be a complete, readable sentence. A reader should know exactly what the answer means without needing to look back at the prompt (it must be self-contained!).
    ❌ Too brief: "$20 million."
    ✅ Self-Contained: "The absolute difference between Microsoft's Intelligent Cloud operating income and Apple's Services operating income was $20 million."
  3. Be fanatical about units and formats
    SEC filings love to play tricks with units (e.g., writing "45" at the top of a table that says in millions). Your Perfect Answer must state the true number with absolute clarity.
  4. Always try to ground your answer in time
    Even if your prompt mentions the year, your Perfect Answer must explicitly restate the specific year, quarter, or date the data belongs to.

IMPORTANT: By convention, for this project, any percentage data must be rounded to the nearest first decimal place and money figures must be rounded to the nearest integer.

HOW TO PROVIDE SUPPORTING FACTS

In the future, other raters will need to verify your answer WITHOUT HAVING TO READ THE ENTIRE SEC filings from scratch. Your Supporting Facts are the only available information they get. If you don't provide the Supporting Facts, the rater will not be able to fulfill their task!

In the task workflow, you will see a specific form to fill out for each fact you need to prove your answer. You can add as many facts as you need by clicking the "Add" button.

Multi-Document Mapping

Unlike the prior project, since you are pulling evidence from two or more completely different PDFs, the rater needs to know exactly which files are mapped to which Supporting Fact. Therefore, you must ALWAYS provide at least ONE Supporting Fact for EACH assigned document. Use the Document Source dropdown to tag where your fact came from.

You will always need to fill the following fields:

  1. Document Source
    Select the exact document (e.g. Document 1, Document 2) from which you extracted this fact.
  2. Evidence (Verbatim Copy-Paste Only!)
    You must highlight the exact text in the PDF; press Ctrl+C, and paste it into this box. Do not summarize it. Do not change the wording.
    TABLE EXCEPTION: It's okay to use brackets "[]" to add the column header context along with the verbatim row content, so that it makes sense. Example: "iPad 28,023 5% 26,694 [Product, 2025, Change, 2024]".
  3. Page Number
    Use the actual printed page number on the document itself.
  4. Page Section
    e.g., "Item 7. Management's Discussion and Analysis".
  5. Element Type
    Paragraph, Table, Footnote, Heading / Subheading, or List / Bullet Points.

WRITING YOUR RATIONALES

The Rationale is the step-by-step logic that connects your raw Supporting Facts to your Perfect Answer. For Category C tasks, this is crucial!

  1. Spell Out the Formula
    Always write out the comparative or financial formula in plain English before plugging the numbers in.
  2. Show the Step-by-Step Math (Including Conversions)
    You must write down the actual arithmetic. If you had to convert units or move a decimal point, show it!
  3. Explain the "Why" (The Hidden Logic)
    Explain your logic as if you are training a junior analyst on their first day.
  4. Entity Labeling (Multi-Document)
    You must clearly label which numbers belong to which Document / Supporting Fact before doing the math, so that raters can follow your logic accurately.
    ❌ Bad: "I took 450M and subtracted 300M." (The rater doesn't know which company made 450M).
    ✅ Good: "Step 1: From Fact 1 (Doc 1), Company X's revenue is 450M. Step 2: From Fact 2 (Doc 2), Company Y's revenue is 300M. Step 3: Calculate the difference (450M - 300M = 150M)."
